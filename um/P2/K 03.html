IRI Overview

 

  1. An Infrared Radiometer Inspection (IRI) is performed on electrical equipment to identify hazards, overloads, load unbalances, areas of energy waste. Typically referred to hot spots thermal activity worsens in time and is responsible for equipment failure and energy waste.
  2. The technician must determine whether any give hot spot is indeed a potential hazard. Ammeter readings are essential because without them, you cannot determine normal operation. If a hot spot is encountered, the tech must photograph the equipment with both digital and infrared camera and give info on location so that others (like repair men) can easily find the equipment
  3. Reports have to be generated following established format.
  4. In the future, we aim to review presentation formats and link data to web based PGI drawings. Also aim at building a library on the normal operation of various equipment types.

  2. When power is quantified, it is usually represented in watts or Kilowatts. Engineering 201 (Richard)

 

Basic Building Systems

 

  1. Electric Service Power is provided to the building from Con Edison by way of the electric service equipment. Services range up to 4,000 amperes in capacity, either at 208 or 480 volts. Many buildings have more than one service. Each service is connected to a service switch or combination of switches which feed distribution circuits that power the building. These circuits are called risers for obvious reasons.

 

  1. Risers consist either of cables in conduit or bus duct which consists of copper or aluminum bars in a metal enclosure. They usually rise through the electric rooms or closets on each floor where cables are tapped in junction boxes or bus duct is tapped by plug-in switches.

 

  1. Tenant Lighting & Power --Generally L&P is furnished via the closest EC or the EC dedicated for a specific area of the building i.e: north & south closets. Tenant L&P therefore is generally available from the floor EC and can be derived from a dedicated distribution or a shared distribution available within the closet. Where the risers are 480 volts transformers are installed in some or all of the electric closets to supply low voltage 120 and 208 volt circuits. A dedicated distribution is generally easier to identify and quantify. With shared distributions there are many more considerations especially where there are both high and low voltage. A Landlords distribution is, of course, shared among building loads and tenants. Hopefully, tenant L&P distribution is not shared with building mechanical distribution.

 

  1. MeteringMetering is either the main utility metering, tenant metering, or landlord metering for purposes of monitoring and hopefully maximizing energy use. There is always a Con Edison metering setup at the point of service; the Con Edison meter(s) measure energy consumption (kwh) and power demand (kw). There is a charge for each kwh consumed and for each kw of demand. One or both of these charges is usually higher in the summer when electricity demand is high. Tenant or landlord meters are installed to allocate the Con Edison charges in proportion to the amount of electricity used. Meters are identified by number, assignment, location, and items metered. The most important thing to remember about metering is that it is worthless unless the equipment is reliable and installed properly. And equipment reliability is easier to find than installation reliability. Meters are not required where the electricity is included in the rent.

 

  1. Mechanical Equipment By mechanical equipment we generally mean heating and cooling systems. Examples of heating systems include: Steam distribution, hydronic distribution, electric duct and baseboard heaters, gas-fired unit heaters, heat pumps. Examples of cooling systems include: Chilled water distribution, central air conditioning and distribution, split and packaged air-conditioning systems (air- and water-cooled), heat pumps. Ancillary equipment that supports the heating and cooling equipment or perform other functions include: cooling towers, heat exchangers, pumps, supply and exhaust fans (for fresh air), and ventilation systems (toilet and kitchen exhausts).

 

  1. Elevator EquipmentElevator equipment is generally on its own switchboard distribution and each elevator machine room typically has its own motor disconnect switches. In many cases elevator motors are still powered via DC power making the entire system easier to identify and separate during the PGI process

 

  1. EmergencyAll buildings have some sort of emergency system. From lighting fixtures and exit signs that house emergency batteries to complex generator systems and UPS systems. Emergency power is generally classified in one of two ways, Life Safety Systems or reliability redundancy. The NYC code requires that high rise and certain other buildings have an emergency power system and an emergency power source and fuel supply sufficient to operate for a period of six hours:

 

      1. Fire pumps and booster pumps.
      2. Three elevators at one time.
      3. Alarm systems.
      4. Communication systems.
      5. Emergency lighting, stair lighting.
      6. Smoke venting systems.
      7. Stair pressurization systems.

 

  1. Fire Alarm - All commercial and industrial buildings have fire alarm systems. Complexities range from simple local evacuation systems with manual pull stations to complex computer controlled addressable systems that continuously supervise the operating status of each device. Generally, in New York City, 100 foot high and taller buildings have what is designated as Class E systems which have warden phones, strobe lights, audible alarms, pull stations, detection equipment, elevator recall and connection to a central station (not the Fire Dept.). This is a tricky item when doing tenant premises because the landlord usually requires upgrade of the fire alarm system to comply with more stringent recent requirements.

 

  1. PlumbingAll buildings require a potable water supply and means for removing sanitary and storm wastewater. Water is supplied by the city through a metered entrance below street level. This water is then distributed via one or more sets of pipe risers throughout the building by one of the following: existing city water pressure (pressure subject to seasonal variations), an elevated house water tank (usually located on the roof building pumps lift the water from the entrance to this level), or domestic water booster pumps (boost the incoming city pressure so that water can be supplied to high floors). Some of the domestic water is diverted for providing hot water (e.g. hot water heater, tankless coil). Building drainage for commercial and residential buildings usually falls under two categories: sanitary and storm. Sanitary drainage includes sinks, toilets, bathtubs, dishwashers, and laundry. Mechanical waste water from cooling towers, boilers, etc, are also diverted to sanitary drains. Storm drains are located at the roof and handle rain accumulation. The storm and sanitary drains usually combine after the sanitary waste has gone through a house trap, and before leaving the building to combine with the city sewer system. Other important plumbing systems include sprinklers (wet and dry types), fire hose standpipes, compressed air (for mechanical equipment control), natural gas, fuel oil, and mechanical-related piping such as steam, heating hot water, condenser water, and chilled water.

Metering 101 (George/Giuseppe)

 

Billing Overview

 

A meter is a devise that measures. Different things are measured using different mechanisms and different units. For example, liquid can be measured in oz., weight in pounds, gas in cubic feet and so on. Electricity is measured in peak watts (or peak KW) and watt hours (or KWHR). Similarly, water can be measured in gallons or cubic feet. Regardless, it is converted to units of 100 cubit feet of HFC (hundreds of Cubic Feet), because the price of water is based on 1 HFC unit.

 

Examples:

Electricity is billed in KW and KWHR with each unit having an associated cost. The cost is determined by the Utility Rate which is applied to a billing period.. So, if in January 1st you turned on a 2000 watts load and you kept it on over a period of 2 hours you will have used a peak KW 2 and a KWHR usage of 4. If a KW unit cost you $20 and a KWHR unit cost you $0.10, then your cost would be 2KW x $20 = $40 and 4KWHR x $0.10 = $0.40: your bill would be $40.40 plus applicable surcharges and taxes.

 

Water is billed in HCF. A single HCF costs $1.31 (plus 159% sewer). So, if you used 4750 cubic feet, you would be billed for 475 HCF (4750/100) and your cost would be 475 HCF x $1.31 = $622.25 plus applicable surcharges and taxes.

 

Gas is billed in therms and Steam is billed in pounds.

 

 

Reading Acquisition

 

Readings are acquired in one of two ways either by a meter reader going to site or electronically.

 

Meter Reader

When a reader goes to a site, he/she will either bring back a sheet of paper with the written readings or he/she will bring back a hand held device containing an electronic file. The file is better because there is no manual entry and we save time. However, regardless of how the reading are acquired, they are generally made up of a running KWHR value and a Peak KW value which is re-set at the end of the period. The reading sheet or the file look like this:

Meter no. KWHR no. KW no. Date Location

We therefore have a lump sum KWHR value and a Lump sum KW value taken on a current date. The current KWHR value is subtracted from the previous KWHR value to establish usage for this period and the KW value is taken as is; remember that it is reset every month.

Your bill would look like this:

 

Meter no. Previous KWHR Current KWHR KWHR KW

1234 15,786 28,961 13,175 20

13,175 x $0.10 = $1,317.50

20 x $20 = $400.00

 

Because from these lump sum values we cannot determine when electricity was used, this format may not hold for very long in a deregulated market if electricity is to have different pricing structures for different time intervals.

 

Electronically

There are of course metering system that are electronically read and yet display data in lump sum running values. These systems though are generally older versions. More common reading systems store data in time intervals so that a KWHR reading is actually the sum of many time intervals and a peak KW reading is actually the single highest interval.

Meter no. Time Date Watt Hours

1234 12:30 1/3 400

1234 12:45 1/3 300

1234 13:00 1/3 300

1234 13:15 1/3 500

Your total usage in this time interval would be 1500 watt hours or 1.5KWHR and your KW would be the highest interval x 4 because there are 4 15 minute intervals in one hour. So, 500 x 4 = 2000 or 2KW

 

Electronic data formatted in this fashion can be trended with a more accurate time definition giving the user a clearer idea of how electricity is used, when it used and when the peaks are occurring. In a deregulated market, understanding and controlling your Peak KW will be essential.

When installing the system, or when installing meter in a building that has a communication system, you must be aware of two things. First there is the meter portion of the work. That is, the actual meter installation. Second, there is the electronic installation. In comparison, the actual meter installation is easy. The Electronic installation instead has several levels before it is complete. First, meters have to be hard wired to the pulse collector. Once the connection is complete, one portion of the electronic link is established. In the above example, meter 123 is connected to Pulse Tracker 101. Therefore, PT 102, channel 1 contains data from meter 123. Otherwise represented as 101-1-m#123. Once that the site installers have connected the wiring from meter to PTs, the above addressable codes have benn established. With these codes, two things must be performed.

(1). The system has to be set up. This can be done locally with a laptop or remotely via dial up. Either way, once the system is turned on and you are connected, all of the PT serial numbers installed should report. Each PT channel can then be set up with meter number (exactly as set up in Utility Manager) and other pertinent info.

(2). A script file must be written. A script file is nothing more than a computerized routine that will automatically perform certain routine functions. Our script files have instructions for the PC to dial up a telephone number (our site) and gather info from various PTs and channels. The gathered info is organized in a particular format and saved electronically with a file name that denotes a building or site address.

 

When the import routine is executed, the operator must select the file from the Utility Manager import facility. Once the file to be imported is select, the file type is chosen (so that Utility Manager recognized pertinent fields), and the building number for which the import is being performed. The routine will Compile the file KWHR data and the KW data by meter no. and place that data in the Utility Manager tables for that building and that meter no. . During this process, coincidental demand is established or the meter is profiled depending on setup parameters.

Energy Services is the first to determine that meter readings have not been imported or have imported incorrect data or no data. Energy Services personnel should then determine whether the problem is electronic or physical. This is easy to determine if you know how to trouble shoot the system. First, you must look at the script file drawn from the building and find your meter number. Then you scroll through the time intervals to determine a discrepancy or a time gap indication a loss of data. You address those errors. If the meter is not in your file, it is one of three things. It is either not connected on the field, connected on the field but not set up in the system, connected on the field, set up in the system, but not written in the script file. Energy services should provide this info to Tech services before tech services begins trouble shooting.
