Overview

A meter is a devise that measures.   Different things are measured using different mechanisms and different units.  For example, liquid can be measured in oz., weight in pounds, gas in cubic feet and so on.  Water can be measured in gallons or cubic feet.  Regardless, water usage readings are converted to units of 100 cubit feet of HFC (hundreds of Cubic Feet), because the price of water in NYC is based on 1 HFC unit.  Electricity is measured in peak watts (aka, peak KW or peak Demand) and watt hours (or KWHR) because pricing is established based on KW and/or KWHR units

 

Examples:

Electricity is billed in KW and KWHR with each unit having an associated cost.  The cost is determined by the Utility Rate which is applied to a billing period.…. So, if in January 1st you turned on a 2000 watts load and you kept it on over a period of 2 hours you will have used a peak KW of 2 and a KWHR usage of 4.  If a KW unit cost you $20 and a KWHR unit cost you $0.10, then your cost would be 2KW x $20 = $40 and 4KWHR x $0.10 = $0.40: your bill would be $40.40 plus applicable surcharges and taxes.

 

Water is billed in HCF.  A single HCF costs $1.31 (plus 159% sewer).  So, if you used 4750 cubic feet, you would be billed for 475 HCF (4750/100) and your cost would be 475 HCF x $1.31 = $622.25 plus applicable surcharges and taxes.

 

Gas is billed in therms and Steam is billed in pounds. 

 

Reading Acquisition-General

Readings are acquired in one of two ways… either by a meter reader going to site or electronically.

 

Meter Reader

When a reader goes to a site, he/she will either bring back a sheet of paper with the written readings or he/she will bring back a hand held device containing an electronic file.  The electronic file is preferred because there is no manual entry required.  Regardless of how these reading are acquired, they are generally made up of a running KWHR value and a max KW value which is re-set at the end of the period.  The reading sheet or the file look like this:

 

Meter no.          KWHR no.       KW no.                        Date     Location

     

We therefore have a lump sum KWHR value and a Lump sum KW value taken on a date pre determined by the utility (utility trip code).  The current  KWHR value is subtracted from the previous KWHR value to establish usage for this period and the KW value is taken as is; remember that it is reset every month.

Your bill would look like this:

 

Meter no.          Previous KWHR                       Current KWHR                        KWHR             KW     

1234                 15,786                                       28,961                                       13,175               20

           

13,175 x $0.10 =            $1,317.50

20 x $20 =$400.00        

 

Because from these lump sum usage values we cannot determine how and when electricity was used, this format may not hold for very long in a deregulated market if electricity is to have different pricing structures for different time intervals.  Manually acquired readings therefore do not lend themselves to data analysis.

 

Electronically

There are of course metering system that are electronically read and yet display data in lump sum running values.  These systems though are generally older versions.  More common reading systems store data in time intervals so that a KWHR reading is actually the sum of many time intervals and a peak KW  reading is actually the single highest interval, or highest consecutive intervals, depending on how KW is billed.  Time interval data generally looks like this:

 

Meter no.          Time     Date     Watt Hours

1234                 12:30    1/3              400

1234                 12:45    1/3              300

1234                 13:00    1/3              300

1234                 13:15    1/3              500

 

Your total usage in this time interval would be 1500 watt hours or 1.5KWHR and your KW would be the highest interval  x 4 because there are 4 ‘15 minute intervals’ in one hour. So, 500 x 4 = 2000 or 2KW

 

If this usage were billed on a sliding KW interval, the KW billed would be 800 (the sum of 300 at 13:00hrs and 500 at 13:15hrs) x 2 = 1600 or 1.6KW

 

Electronic data formatted in this fashion can be trended with a more accurate time definition giving the user a clearer idea of how electricity is used, when it used and when the peaks are occurring.  In a deregulated market, understanding and controlling your Peak KW will be essential.

           

Reading Acquisition-Details

Genergy acquires meter readings in one of two ways.  We have a meter reader that goes to the various sites to read meters and we have meters connected to remote systems.  The pros and the difficulties of each method of data acquisition are as follows:

 

Meter Reader

Pros

-Reader is at the building on a monthly basis & hopefully is able to build good relationships, verify conditions, and brings back ‘heads up’ information such as pending construction or tenant relocation.

Difficulty

-Human error and/or lack of interest.  The human error part can be minimized through better tools like a hand held device.  Interest however is also important.  The reader must realize that he/she is the link with the building, is representing Genergy, and that he/she must bring back accurate readings and info.

 

Electronic Files  

Pros

-The acquisition of electronic files eliminates the need for a reader.  Readings therefore are acquired and processed much faster. 

-Because computers ‘do not make mistakes’, erroneous readings are minimized and/or eliminated.

-Readings gathered tend to be more detailed, providing interval data or ‘time/date’ stamped data.  Interval data lends itself to aggregation, time of use analysis, establishing coincidental demand, and profiling.  Interval data is where the industry is headed.

Difficulty

-Building reliable metering systems comprised of reliable equipment and workmanship.   Understanding those systems and trouble shooting those systems efficiently.

-Maintaining systems on line status

-Dealing with electronic files and realizing the sensitivity issues because a ‘0’ is much different than an ‘o’

-Coordinating data traffic and speeding up acquisition/import

-Establishing error handling routines to minimize manual review.

Installation

The PT File, the LM file and the Pulse Dump File are very similar. For one thing, they all display interval data.  The basic difference is that while the PT and Andover files are for every meter on the system, the LM file is 1 file per meter.  From an installation standpoint, the concept is the same. 

 

 

 

When installing any of these systems, or when installing a meter in a building that has a communication system, you must be aware of two things.  First there is the meter portion of the work.  That is, the actual meter installation.  Second, there is the electronic installation.  In comparison, the actual meter installation is easy.  The Electronic installation instead has several levels before it is complete.  First, meters have to be hard wired to the pulse collector.  Once the connection is complete, one portion of the electronic link is established.  In the above example, meter 123 is connected to Pulse Tracker 101channel 1.  Therefore, PT 101, channel 1 contains data from meter 123.  Otherwise represented as 101-1-m#123.  Once that the site installers have connected the wiring from meter to PTs, the above addressable codes have been established.  With these codes, two things must be performed.

 (1).  The system has to be set up.  This can be done locally with a laptop or remotely via dial up. Either way, once the system is turned on and you are connected, all of the PT serial numbers installed should report.  Each PT channel can then be ‘set up’ with meter number (exactly as set up in Utility Manager) and other pertinent info. 

(2).  A script file must be written.  A script file is nothing more than a computerized routine that will automatically perform certain routine functions.  Our script files have instructions for the PC to dial up a telephone number (our site) and gather info from various PT’s and channels.  The gathered info is organized in a particular format and saved electronically with a file name that denotes a building or site address.

 

When the import routine is executed, the operator must select the file from the Utility Manager import facility.  In the future, this step will be eliminated and the file will automatically be imported using other mechanisms.  Once the file to be imported is select, the file type is chosen (so that Utility Manager recognized pertinent fields), and the building number for which the import is being performed.  The routine will Compile the file KWHR data and the KW data by meter no. and place that data in the Utility Manager tables for that building and that meter no. .  During this process, coincidental demand is established or the meter is profiled depending on setup parameters.

Energy Services is the first to determine that meter readings have not been imported or have imported incorrect data or no data.  Energy Services personnel should then determine whether the problem is electronic or physical.  This is easy to determine if you know how to trouble shoot the system.  First, you must look at the script file drawn from the building and find your meter number.  Then you scroll through the time intervals to determine a discrepancy or a time gap indication a loss of data.  You address those errors.  If the meter is not in your file, it is one of three things…. It is either not connected on the field, connected on the field but not set up in the system, connected on the field, set up in the system, but not written in the script file.  Energy services should provide this info to Tech services before tech services begins trouble shooting.

 

Start Up Reports

Start Up Reports are issued by Genergy whenever any metering work is performed.  The report serves as notice to the client that a meter work has taken place and that work has been checked, verified and accepted by our technicians.  Start Up Reports are also necessary internally because they inform the account manager, aka biller, that meter work has taken place. 

 

The steps required during the Start Up process are enumerated below in chronological order:

 

In case of a new meter installation:

 

1.       A meter is installed and connected to the remote system, if such a system is present.

2.       The meter is checked by our field technicians to ensure that it is operational and has been installed properly.  If the meter is connected to a system, the system will be programmed and reading acquisition will be verified.  The meter will then be ‘set up’ in Utility Manager under tenant ‘unassigned meters’ 

3.       A ‘Partial’ Start Up Report is issued internally by the field technicians informing the billers that a new meter has been added.   The technician will issue the lease clause as an attachment to the partial start up. 

4.       The field technician will acquire manual readings on the meter for at least 1 full billing cycle and forward those readings to the biller who will compare those manual readings to the imported readings, if a system is present.

5.       If manual readings and import reading show that the meter is operating properly, the biller will distribute a final Start Up internally to the client.

6.       3 months later, and the interim charge is calculated per client’s request.

 

In case of a reassigned meter scenario:

 .

7.       The meter is checked by our field technicians to ensure that it is operational and is installed properly.  If the meter is connected to a system, the system will be reprogrammed and reading acquisition will be verified.  Existing meter ‘assignment’ and ‘on line’ status will be checked by the technician for the meter in question.  The meter will be reassigned and/or rendered active by the technician. 

8.       A ‘Partial’ Start Up Report is issued internally by the field technicians informing the billers that a meter has been reassigned.   The technician will issue the lease clause as an attachment to the partial start up. 

9.       If latest  historical reading are not available, the field technician will acquire manual readings on the meter for at least 1 full billing cycle and forward those readings to the biller who will compare those manual readings to the imported readings, if a system is present.

10.   If manual readings and import reading show that the meter is operating properly, the biller will distribute a final Start Up internally to the client.

11.   3 months later, and the interim charge is calculated per client’s request.
